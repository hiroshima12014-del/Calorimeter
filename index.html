📋 Original Experiment

Source: Pearson Chemistry for Queensland Units 1 & 2 — Investigating energy change in chemical reactions

The original experiment dissolves a fixed mass of a salt in a fixed volume of water inside a simple polystyrene-cup calorimeter, recording the temperature change to calculate the enthalpy of solution (ΔH). The experiment determines whether the dissolution is exothermic or endothermic and computes a single value of ΔH for comparison against a literature value.

Your modification: You replace the polystyrene cup with a aluminium calorimeter wrapped in polystyrene insulation for improved heat transfer characteristics, vary your chosen independent variable across multiple levels with multiple trials each, and use Q = mcΔT together with the mole concept (n = m/M) to calculate molar enthalpy of solution — turning a single-point measurement into a quantitative investigation with experimental versus theoretical comparison.

⚗ What is a Calorimeter?

A calorimeter is an insulated vessel used to measure heat changes during a chemical or physical process. In this investigation, a 150 mL aluminium calorimeter with an inner styrofoam liner and a transparent lid holds a known mass of water; the salt is added through a port in the lid and the temperature change of the water is recorded using a Vernier temperature probe. The styrofoam liner sits inside the aluminium outer can and is what directly contacts the water — this decouples the water from the aluminium's larger thermal mass and provides excellent insulation. The transparent lid prevents heat loss by convection and evaporation, while ports allow access for the probe and stirrer.

⚠ Polystyrene must not be used in any experiment involving open flames — it ignites readily and produces toxic styrene vapour. For this calorimetry setup the polystyrene only wraps the outside of the aluminium can; no flame is present.

⚡ Thermochemical Equation

Dissolution equation
Literature ΔHsolution
The sign of ΔH tells you whether the process is endothermic (+, absorbs heat from water → water cools) or exothermic (−, releases heat to water → water warms). The magnitude tells you how much energy per mole is exchanged.

🔗 The Mechanism: Lattice → Hydration → ΔH

When an ionic salt dissolves, two competing energy changes occur. The relationship between them determines whether the overall process is endothermic or exothermic.

Step 1 — Lattice breaks (endothermic)
Energy is required to overcome the electrostatic attractions holding the ions in the crystal lattice: ΔHlattice > 0. The stronger the lattice (small, highly charged ions), the more energy required.
Step 2 — Ions are hydrated (exothermic)
Water molecules surround the separated ions and form ion–dipole attractions. Energy is released as these new attractions form: ΔHhydration < 0. Small, highly charged ions release more energy when hydrated.
Step 3 — Overall enthalpy of solution
ΔHsolution = ΔHlattice + ΔHhydration. If lattice energy > total hydration energy → endothermic (water cools). If hydration energy > lattice energy → exothermic (water warms).
Connection to bond enthalpy: This is the same concept as bond enthalpy — breaking attractions absorbs energy, forming attractions releases energy. But the syllabus dot point about "limitations of average bond enthalpies" applies here: average bond enthalpies are defined for covalent bonds in gas-phase reactions, so they cannot be plugged directly into a dissolution calculation. This is a key limitation worth discussing in your evaluation.

📐 Calculation Pathway

1. Mass of water from volume: m = V × ρ (ρ_water ≈ 1.00 g/mL)
2. Temperature change: ΔT = T_final − T_initial
3. Heat exchanged with water: Q = m × c × ΔT (c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹)
4. Moles of salt: n = m_salt / M_salt
5. Molar enthalpy of solution: ΔH = −Q / n (sign convention: endothermic dissolution means water lost heat → Q negative for water → ΔH positive for the salt)
6. Statistics across trials: mean · AU = (max − min)/2 · PU(%) = AU/mean × 100
7. Propagated uncertainty: %U(ΔH) = %U(m_w) + %U(ΔT) + %U(m_salt)
8. Percentage error: |ΔH_exp − ΔH_lit| / |ΔH_lit| × 100
9. Percentage yield: ΔH_exp / ΔH_lit × 100
Constants: cwater = 4.18 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹ | ρwater = 1.00 g/mL | Convention: ΔH > 0 endothermic, ΔH < 0 exothermic

🔬 Sign Convention — Important!

Students commonly get confused about the sign of ΔH. The convention is:

Endothermic dissolution (e.g. NH₄Cl)
Water loses heat to the dissolving salt → water temperature falls (ΔT < 0) → Q for water is negative → heat absorbed by the salt is +Q → ΔHsolution is positive.
Exothermic dissolution (e.g. CaCl₂)
Water gains heat from the dissolving salt → water temperature rises (ΔT > 0) → Q for water is positive → heat released by the salt is −Q → ΔHsolution is negative.
Practical formula: Compute ΔH = −Qwater / nsalt. The negative sign flips the perspective from "heat gained by water" to "heat absorbed by the dissolving salt".

Uncertainty & trends
Systematic error

Pushes all results in the same direction — consistently too high or too low. The trend may look clean but the whole dataset is biased. Evidence: ΔHexp sits consistently below (or above) ΔHlit across all levels. Fix: change method or apparatus.

Random error

Causes scatter between repeated trials. Values jump above and below the true value due to small, uncontrollable differences each time. Evidence: large AU and PU(%) values; scatter around the trend line. Fix: repeat trials and standardise technique.

🧠 Which errors are strongest in this investigation?

The most defensible major errors for this investigation are heat loss/gain through the calorimeter, thermometer precision (particularly at small ΔT), and the calorimeter thermal mass. The first two affect different IV levels differently — heat loss is a constant proportional drain, while thermometer precision dominates at small ΔT.

Good evaluation move: pick one or two errors that best explain your specific data. Classify each, link to your AU/PU(%)/% error values, and propose a realistic improvement for an insulated aluminium calorimeter — not a generic "use better equipment".
1

Heat loss / gain through the calorimeter walls

Systematic Underestimates |ΔH|
Three leak paths remain. (1) Heat through the lid ports around the probe and stirrer. (2) Heat through the plastic lid itself — it was designed for visibility, not insulation, and conducts heat noticeably better than polystyrene foam. (3) Slow conduction through the styrofoam side walls to the aluminium outer can. Combined, these typically produce <10% deviation from the true ΔT — much better than an open beaker (20–30%), but not negligible.

The styrofoam liner and transparent lid reduce heat exchange significantly compared to an uninsulated container, but they don't eliminate it. Three leak paths remain. Heat leaks through the lid ports (around the temperature probe and stirrer). The styrofoam itself slowly transfers heat between the water and the aluminium outer can. And — critically — the lid is thin transparent plastic, not polystyrene foam: plastic conducts heat noticeably better than foam, so heat moves through the lid material itself, not just through the port gaps. For endothermic dissolutions (water cools), warmth from the room leaks back into the water through these paths, making ΔT smaller than it should be. For exothermic dissolutions (water warms), heat escapes from the water in the same way. In both cases the measured magnitude of ΔH is biased below the true value — much smaller than for an uninsulated open beaker, but not zero.

  • Why this apparatus is partially vulnerable: the styrofoam liner is an excellent insulator on the sides, but the plastic lid is not — it was designed for visibility, not thermal isolation. The lid ports for the probe and stirrer are also not sealed. Over the timescale of dissolution (60–90 s), measurable heat exchange occurs by all three paths.
  • Compared to the original beaker setup: dissolving the salt in an open beaker (the experiment performed in class) has no lid, no styrofoam liner, and full convective/evaporative loss from a large water surface. Moving to the calorimeter typically reduces heat-loss error from 20–30% (open beaker) down to under 10% (calorimeter) — a substantial improvement that justifies the methodological change.
  • Evidence: ΔHexp consistently smaller in magnitude than ΔHlit → percentage yield slightly below 100% (typically <10% deviation for this apparatus, vs >20% with an open beaker).
  • Improvement: place a layer of polystyrene foam over the plastic lid to insulate the top of the calorimeter (visibility is lost, but heat loss through the lid material is largely eliminated); seal the lid ports more tightly around the probe and stirrer with foam plugs or rubber gaskets; reduce the time between salt addition and reading T_final by using powdered salt; record the temperature continuously with the Vernier probe and extrapolate back to t = 0 to correct for heat exchange during dissolution.
2

Calorimeter thermal mass (styrofoam liner absorbs/releases heat)

Systematic Slightly underestimates |ΔH|
The styrofoam liner directly contacts the water and absorbs/releases a small amount of heat as the water changes temperature. The aluminium outer can is thermally isolated from the water by the foam, so its much larger thermal mass does not directly contribute on the short timescale of dissolution. The total error is small (~1.5%), much less than for a metal-walled calorimeter.

The styrofoam liner is what directly contacts the water inside this calorimeter. When the water changes temperature, the inner surface of the styrofoam changes with it, drawing a small amount of heat from (or releasing it to) the water. Because Q = mwcΔT only counts the water's own heat capacity, this small slice is missing from the calculation, biasing |ΔH| low. The aluminium outer can has a much larger total thermal mass than the styrofoam, but it is thermally isolated from the water by the foam, so on the short timescale of dissolution (60–90 s) it does not directly contribute.

  • Magnitude: styrofoam has c ≈ 1.3 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹ (high per gram, like most polymers), but the liner itself is light (typically only a few grams). A 5 g liner contributes ~6.5 J/°C, compared to 418 J/°C for 100 g of water — a ratio of about 1.5%.
  • For comparison: the aluminium outer can (c = 0.897 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹) would contribute much more if it directly contacted the water, but the styrofoam liner prevents this on the experimental timescale. This apparatus is designed specifically to keep the thermal-mass error small.
  • Evidence: ΔHexp systematically below ΔHlit by a small, roughly constant percentage across trials — typically less than 2% if heat exchange is also well controlled.
  • Improvement: calibrate the calorimeter heat capacity by mixing measured volumes of hot and cold water and measuring the equilibrium temperature; apply this as a correction term in calculations (Q_total = (mwcw + Ccal) × ΔT). For typical Year 11 work, the error is small enough that this calibration is usually not necessary.
3

Thermometer precision and parallax

Random Affects ΔT both ways
The Vernier probe used here has an accuracy of ±0.25 °C, doubled to ±0.5 °C for the ΔT difference. At small ΔT (large water volumes, weakly endothermic salts like NaCl, or small salt masses), this 0.5 °C absolute uncertainty is a substantial fraction of ΔT — it can easily exceed all other propagated uncertainties combined.

The temperature probe used in this experiment is a Vernier Stainless-Steel Temperature Probe with the following specifications: 0.01 °C resolution (smallest increment shown), ±0.25 °C accuracy (closeness to true value), and a response time of ~20 seconds in stirred water. Continuous logging at up to 10 Hz removes the need to identify the temperature extremum visually. Because ΔT is the difference of two readings, the uncertainty in ΔT is conventionally taken as 2 × the single-reading uncertainty, giving ±0.5 °C. When ΔT itself is small (large water volumes, small salt masses, or weakly endothermic salts like NaCl), this ±0.5 °C absolute uncertainty dominates the propagated uncertainty in ΔH.

  • Worked example (this apparatus): a 120 mL trial of 5 g NH₄Cl gives ΔT ≈ −2.4 °C, so %U(ΔT) = 2 × 0.25 / 2.4 × 100 = 20.8% — already a substantial slice of the propagated uncertainty.
  • Worked example (compared to a glass thermometer): the same ΔT = −2.4 °C with a glass thermometer (±0.5 °C) gives 2 × 0.5 / 2.4 × 100 = 41.7% — twice the Vernier value. So even with the Vernier's ±0.25 °C accuracy, the probe upgrade halves the thermometer's contribution to total uncertainty.
  • Note on systematic vs random: the ±0.25 °C accuracy bundles both systematic offset (calibration error) and random precision. For ΔT specifically, systematic offset largely cancels (same probe for both readings), so the true random uncertainty in ΔT is often smaller than the conservative 2 × 0.25 = ±0.5 °C estimate. Year 11 evaluations should use the conservative value but can note this in a discussion of uncertainty.
  • Evidence: large PU(%) at IV levels where ΔT is small (high water volume, low salt mass); systematically large %U(ΔH) for NaCl trials compared to other salts.
  • Improvement: ensure ΔT is as large as possible — choose strongly endothermic/exothermic salts (NH₄Cl, KCl, CaCl₂) over weak ones (NaCl), use smaller water volumes within the IV range, and use larger salt masses when not the IV. A higher-accuracy probe (e.g. platinum RTD ±0.05 °C) would also reduce this error, but is unusual in school labs.
4

Incomplete dissolution within the measurement window

Systematic Underestimates |ΔH|
If the salt does not fully dissolve before T_final is recorded, the calculation assumes all the weighed mass was responsible for the observed heat change — when in fact only the dissolved portion was. The result is a systematic underestimate of |ΔH| per mole.

If the salt does not fully dissolve before the final temperature reading is taken, the calculated ΔH (which assumes all of the weighed mass dissolved) is biased. This is particularly likely at higher salt masses, lower water volumes, or with less soluble salts. The system has not absorbed (or released) the full quantity of heat associated with complete dissolution.

  • Why this apparatus is vulnerable: stirring is by hand with a glass rod in an open-topped aluminium can. Stirring is inconsistent between trials and between students.
  • Evidence: visible undissolved crystals at the bottom of the calorimeter at the end of a trial; ΔHexp smaller in magnitude than expected, particularly at high mass / low volume levels.
  • Improvement: use finely powdered salt rather than crystals; stir continuously and consistently; extend the measurement time; ensure salt mass is well below the solubility limit at the test temperature.

5 Specific heat capacity assumption

Systematic · small

The calculation uses c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹ (pure water), but the actual solution has dissolved salt which slightly lowers c. The effect is typically < 2% at the concentrations used here.

  • Evidence: hard to isolate from other systematic errors; usually small enough to ignore.
  • Improvement: use solution-specific c values from a data book (Year 12 extension).

6 Evaporation

Systematic · very small with lid

Water evaporating from the surface carries heat away as latent heat of vaporisation. The transparent lid largely prevents this — evaporated water condenses on the underside of the lid and returns to the bulk water. The only remaining loss is through the lid ports around the probe and stirrer, where water vapour can escape.

  • Evidence: small condensation visible on the inside of the lid during exothermic trials confirms most evaporated water is retained.
  • Improvement: seal the lid ports more tightly to reduce vapour loss through the openings; if the lid is removed during a trial, even briefly, this error becomes much larger.

7 Balance precision & transfer loss

Random + systematic

Balance reads to ±0.01 g (random); some salt may stick to the weighing boat and not transfer into the water (systematic, underestimates mass dissolved → underestimates |ΔH|).

  • Evidence: small contribution to propagated uncertainty at typical 5 g masses (0.2%).
  • Improvement: weigh by difference (weigh boat before and after transfer); use a smooth-surfaced weigh boat or tip directly from a weighing paper.

8 Stirrer friction (viscous heating)

Systematic · very small · biases |ΔH| low

Mechanical energy from stirring dissipates in the water as heat (viscous friction). For gentle hand-stirring with a rod, the power input is roughly 0.01–0.1 W, contributing only 0.6–9 J over a 60–90 s trial — about 0.04–0.7% of the heat budget for a typical 5 g salt dissolution (≈1400 J). For endothermic dissolutions this small heat input partly offsets the water's cooling; for exothermic, it adds to the warming. Either way, |ΔH| is biased slightly low.

  • Evidence: ΔH underestimated by a tiny consistent margin (within combined random scatter). Vigorous stirring increases this; gentle stirring minimises it.
  • Improvement: stir at a consistent gentle rate across all trials; if quantitative control matters (Year 12 extension), measure the stirring contribution by running a "stir-only" blank with no salt added and subtracting the resulting heat input from each trial.

📐 Propagated Uncertainty — Combining Sources

Each measurement has an instrument uncertainty. When you combine measurements in a calculation, the uncertainties propagate through the result. For products and quotients (like Q = mcΔT and ΔH = Q/n), the percentage uncertainties add:

%U(ΔH) = %U(mwater) + %U(ΔT) + %U(msalt)

where each %U is the instrument uncertainty divided by the measured value, times 100. Note that %U(ΔT) is doubled because ΔT is itself a difference of two readings, each with the thermometer's uncertainty. Constants (c, M) have no uncertainty.

Why this matters in your evaluation: compare your % error against the propagated %U. If % error ≤ propagated %U, the experimental and literature values agree within measurement uncertainty (no systematic error needed to explain the gap). If % error > propagated %U, measurement uncertainty cannot explain the gap — there must be systematic error. This is the strongest analytical move you can make.

📈 Trendlines, R², and Gradient Uncertainty

When your IV is quantitative (water volume or salt mass), the QCAA syllabus expects you to draw a line of best fit, report its equation and R² value, and draw maximum and minimum trendlines through the error-bar extremes of your data to quantify the uncertainty in the gradient.

Line of Best Fit

The regression line minimising squared distance from all data points. Written as y = mx + c where m is the gradient.

R² — Coefficient of Determination

Fraction of variation in DV explained by IV. R² ≥ 0.95 = strong linear relationship.

Max/Min Gradient Lines

Lines drawn through the top of the first error bar to the bottom of the last (max), and bottom of first to top of last (min). Gradient uncertainty = (mmax − mmin) / 2.

Hypothesis-Test Graphs

For ΔH vs IV (which should be flat), the "best fit" is the mean across levels; max/min lines bracket the mean ± AU. A gradient distinguishable from zero indicates a real IV dependence.

In your report: toggle Max/Min lines on in the Graphs tab using the button at the top, then quote: "The gradient of the line of best fit was [m] ± [uncertainty] [units], with R² = [value], indicating a [strong/moderate] linear relationship."

1 General thermochemistry theory 0 words ▾
Define heat as a form of energy, temperature as average kinetic energy of particles, and distinguish exothermic from endothermic reactions. Cite at least one source.
Heat is a form of energy transferred between substances at different temperatures, and temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance. Chemical reactions are classified as exothermic if they release heat to the surroundings (causing the surroundings to warm), and endothermic if they absorb heat from the surroundings (causing the surroundings to cool). The enthalpy change of a reaction (ΔH) is the heat exchanged with the surroundings at constant pressure: ΔH > 0 for endothermic processes and ΔH < 0 for exothermic processes.
2 Energy of dissolution — lattice and hydration 0 words ▾
Explain why dissolving an ionic salt involves both an energy-absorbing step (breaking the ionic lattice) and an energy-releasing step (water hydrating the ions), and how their balance determines the sign of ΔHsolution. Link this to the bond enthalpy concept.
ionic lattice electrostatic attraction lattice energy hydration energy ion–dipole attraction endothermic exothermic bond breaking absorbs energy bond formation releases energy
When an ionic compound dissolves in water, the crystal lattice must first be broken apart, which requires energy to overcome the electrostatic attractions between oppositely charged ions (an endothermic step). The separated ions are then surrounded by water molecules, forming ion–dipole attractions in a process called hydration; this step releases energy (exothermic). The overall enthalpy of solution is the sum of these two effects: ΔH_solution = ΔH_lattice + ΔH_hydration. If the lattice energy exceeds the total hydration energy, dissolution is endothermic overall; if hydration energy dominates, dissolution is exothermic. This follows the same principle as average bond enthalpy — breaking attractions absorbs energy, forming new attractions releases it — although bond enthalpy values themselves are defined for covalent bonds in the gas phase and are not directly applied to ionic dissolution.
⚠ Common error: writing "the bonds in water break" — water's covalent bonds are NOT broken during dissolution. Only ion–dipole attractions form and ionic bonds break.
3 Q = mcΔT and the mole concept 0 words ▾
Introduce Q = mcΔT as the bridge between observable temperature change and heat exchanged. Define the mole and link mass to moles via n = m/M. State ΔH = Q/n with attention to sign.
specific heat capacity cwater = 4.18 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹ Q = mcΔT n = m/M Avogadro's number molar enthalpy intensive property ΔH = −Q / n
The heat absorbed or released by a known mass of water is calculated using Q = mcΔT, where m is the water mass, c is its specific heat capacity (4.18 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹), and ΔT is the temperature change. A mole is the amount of substance containing 6.022 × 10²³ representative particles (Avogadro's number). The mole concept connects mass to amount via n = m / M, where M is the molar mass of the substance. The molar enthalpy of solution is calculated as ΔH = −Q_water / n_salt. The negative sign reflects that heat lost by the water has been absorbed by the dissolving salt (and vice versa), giving the correct sign for the salt's perspective. ΔH is an intensive property — it does not depend on the amount of material reacting. The same dissolution should yield the same ΔH per mole regardless of how much salt or water is used (within experimental uncertainty).
4 Mechanism linking your IV to the DV 0 words ▾
Explain exactly how your chosen IV connects to the calculated ΔH. This is the heart of your rationale. If your IV is water volume or salt mass, the logical prediction is that ΔH (per mole) should remain constant because ΔH is intensive — this is a hypothesis-test design. If your IV is salt identity, predict the rank-order based on lattice/hydration energies.
When the volume of water increases at fixed salt mass, the same moles of salt absorb (or release) the same total quantity of heat Q. Because Q = mcΔT with constant Q and increasing m, ΔT must decrease proportionally. However, since ΔH = −Q/n divides by the same number of moles, the calculated ΔH per mole should remain constant across all water volumes. When the mass of salt increases at fixed water volume, the moles of dissolving salt increase. More moles release (or absorb) more total heat, so ΔT is expected to increase linearly with mass. When this heat is divided by moles to give ΔH per mole, the result should be approximately constant — testing whether the method is internally consistent. The sign and magnitude of ΔH_solution depend on the balance of lattice and hydration energies for each salt. Salts with strong lattice energies relative to their hydration energies (e.g. NH₄Cl, KCl) will be endothermic; salts whose ions hydrate strongly (small, highly charged ions like Ca²⁺ in CaCl₂) will be exothermic.
5 Explicit directional prediction 0 words ▾
State your specific, testable prediction. For hypothesis-test designs, this is a statement of constancy ("ΔH per mole will remain approximately constant within experimental uncertainty"). For categorical IVs, predict the rank-order of values you'll observe.
Therefore, it is hypothesised that the calculated molar enthalpy of solution will remain approximately constant across the five [water volumes / salt masses] tested, agreeing within propagated experimental uncertainty with the literature value of [literature ΔH] kJ/mol. It is predicted that the change in water temperature (ΔT) will [decrease as water volume increases / increase linearly with salt mass], following the relationship ΔT = Q / (m_water × c) at constant Q. The deviation of experimental ΔH from the literature value at each level provides a measure of systematic error in the apparatus, while consistency across levels tests the validity of treating ΔH as an intensive property.
Model rationale paragraph — NH₄Cl with water volume IV ▾
"Heat is a form of energy transferred between substances at different temperatures, and is observable as a change in temperature of the surroundings (Pearson, 2019). Chemical and physical changes can absorb or release heat: endothermic processes draw heat from the surroundings (the surroundings cool), while exothermic processes release heat (the surroundings warm). The enthalpy change ΔH quantifies this exchange at constant pressure. When an ionic salt such as ammonium chloride (NH₄Cl) dissolves, the crystal lattice is broken apart against the electrostatic attractions holding the ions in place — an endothermic step. The separated NH₄⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are then surrounded by water molecules, forming ion–dipole attractions in a process called hydration, which releases energy. The overall enthalpy of solution is the sum: ΔH_solution = ΔH_lattice + ΔH_hydration. For NH₄Cl, the literature value is +14.78 kJ/mol (CRC Handbook), indicating that lattice energy slightly exceeds total hydration energy. The heat absorbed by the dissolving salt comes from the surrounding water, which is detected as a temperature drop. This relationship is quantified by Q = mcΔT, where Q is the heat exchanged, m is the mass of water, c is its specific heat capacity (4.18 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹), and ΔT is the temperature change. The moles of NH₄Cl dissolved are calculated using n = m_salt / M_salt, and the molar enthalpy is then ΔH = −Q_water / n_salt. Because ΔH is an intensive property (it does not depend on the amount of substance), it is hypothesised that calculated ΔH will remain approximately constant across the five water volumes tested (50, 100, 150, 200, 250 mL with a fixed 5.00 g of NH₄Cl), agreeing with the literature value of +14.78 kJ/mol within propagated experimental uncertainty. Any systematic deviation from this constancy would indicate experimental error such as heat exchange with the surroundings or thermometer-precision limitations at small ΔT."
